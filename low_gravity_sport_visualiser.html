Low-Gravity Sport Visualiser

A simplified interactive model for three questions: running and braking, catching / horizontal impacts, and lifting / upward projection. The aim is not perfect biomechanics; it is to make the key trade-offs visible: lower gravity reduces weight and frictional grip, while mass and momentum remain unchanged.

Model: the runner can command a horizontal drive or brake force based on muscle capability, but the surface can only transmit up to the friction limit. If the commanded force exceeds μs m g, the foot slips and the usable ground force drops to μk m g. Air drag is included so a true top speed can emerge.

Version 1 ignores toppling and only asks whether the feet can stay planted. The incoming object is assumed to be retained by the receiver. Contact time is estimated from stopping distance; that sets the average contact force and the impulse that the ground must supply if the receiver is not to slide.

Upward force is referenced to Earth bodyweight rather than current local weight, so the same slider can show why “the same athlete” becomes much more explosive in lower gravity. If the upward force is below local weight, there is no lift-off.
Sensible version-2 upgrades would be: toppling in the impact model (centre-of-mass height and support-base width), optional non-retained impacts, and a more biomechanical running model with stride frequency limits.
