Compton Effect: angle, wavelength, target mass, and “different constants”

Uses the Compton shift Δλ = (h / (m c)) (1 − cos θ). Adjust the controls to see why the effect is obvious for X/γ photons, tiny for visible light, and even smaller for protons.

Controls

0° is forward scattering; 180° is backscatter.
Covers γ rays (0.001 nm) through visible (≈400–700 nm) out to IR (10,000 nm).
Electron vs proton mainly changes h/(mc), i.e., the Compton wavelength.
Scale h by powers of ten to see alternate “quantum-ness”.
h = h₀ × 10^k with h₀ = 6.62607015×10⁻³⁴ J·s.
Frequency drop is Δf = f − f′ ≥ 0.
Tip: set λ to around 500 nm and compare electron vs proton on Δf/f to see how tiny it becomes.

Change vs scattering angle

The curve is computed from λ′ = λ + (h/(mc))(1 − cos θ), then f′ = c/λ′. For visible wavelengths, Δλ is vastly smaller than λ, so the plot can look flat unless you use a relative measure or log axis.
